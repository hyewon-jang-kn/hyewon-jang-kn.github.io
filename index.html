Hyewon Jang

About Me

I am a doctoral researcher in computational psycholinguistics at the University of Konstanz, supervised by Diego Frassinelli and Bettina Braun. I use experimental and computational linguistics methods to investigate the pragmatic dimensions of language that make human language complex and fun, with sarcasm being the current topic of interest.

I got into computational psycholinguistics after I received my master's degree in Speech and Language Processing from the University of Konstanz. Before getting serious about research, I was an avid learner and teacher of languages. I got a bachelor's degree in English Education at Seoul National University and worked as an ESL teacher for a while.

Research Interests

Experimental Linguistics

I test my hypotheses about language phenomena by designing controlled experiments for human participants. I also use these experiments to collect data for computational modeling experiments.

Multimodality

Messages embedded in language are conveyed through textual, prosodic, and visual modalities. I involve different modalities in my experiments to investigate their effects on communication.

Computational Linguistics

I investigate the behavior of language models in handling figurative language. I use different computational modeling techniques, from state-of-the-art language models to traditional models.

Figurative Language

Using the above methodologies, I investigate figurative language, mainly sarcasm, irony, or humor. My ultimate goal is to gather knowledge about how humans and computers handle intricate language phenomena and use it to help build better NLP applications.

Peer-Reviewed Publications

Hyewon Jang †, Qi Yu †, Diego Frassinelli, The Inner Workings of Language Models in Figurative Language Processing: Linguistically Informed Feature Analyses of Model Behavior, Findings of the Association for Computational Linguistics: ACL 2023.   [PAPER] [POSTER]
† equal contribution

Summary
In this paper, we probe Transformer-based models and traditional machine learning models to investigate what happens under the hood when these models perform a figurative language classification task. We perform feature importance analyses and compare the behavior of different models on sarcasm, simile, idiom, and metaphor. We also compare model behavior with human behavior in the same task, and provide insight into different complexity levels of different figurative language types.

Hyewon Jang, Bettina Braun, Diego Frassinelli, Intended and Perceived Sarcasm Between Close Friends: What Triggers Sarcasm and What Gets Conveyed? Proceedings of the 45th Annual Conference of the Cognitive Science Society (CogSci 2023).   [PAPER] [POSTER]

Summary
In this paper, we investigate what factors trigger sarcasm between close friends and whether the intentions behind a sarcastic comment are also conveyed to the listener. We answer our research questions using two connected experiments and analyze the results with the linear mixed-effects model.

Other Publications

Hyewon Jang, Lexicon-Based Profiling of Irony and Stereotype Spreaders, CLEF 2022 – Conference and Labs of the Evaluation Forum, September 5–8, 2022, Bologna, Italy.   [PAPER]

Summary
In this working note, I report the results of my experiments on profiling of Twitter authors that spread irony and stereotypes. I also report results from an ablation experiment to identify some of the most informative features for irony and stereotype profiling. This work is the result of my participation in The PAN 22 Author Profiling Shared Task (IROSTEREO).

Ana-Maria Bucur, Hyewon Jang, Farhana Ferdousi Liza, Capturing Changes in Mood Over Time in Longitudinal Data Using Ensemble Methodologies, In Proceedings of the Eighth Workshop on Computational Linguistics and Clinical Psychology, pages 205–212, Seattle, USA. Association for Computational Linguistics.   [PAPER]

Summary
In this working note, we describe our system that identifies changes in mood and behavior in longitudinal text data. This work is the result of our participation in the CLPsych 2022 Shared Task.
